the core thesis

Students are consumers of AI learning tools. How they evaluate, adopt, and interact with these tools is a marketing question — but the outcome variable is learning. This intersection creates a research space where marketing theory (framing, adoption, positioning) generates novel predictions about educational outcomes that education research alone would miss.

POCKET 01

AI Tool Framing × Learning Outcomes: When Positioning Shapes Cognition

"How does the framing of AI learning tools (tutor vs. collaborator vs. answer engine) shape students' usage patterns, cognitive engagement, and downstream learning outcomes — and what psychological mechanisms (growth mindset, self-efficacy, locus of control) mediate this relationship?"

Why this is strong: This is fundamentally a product positioning question — core marketing theory. But the DV is learning, which makes it novel. Bastani et al. showed that guardrails matter; we're asking why and for whom through the lens of how the product is framed to the consumer. This is not replication — it's extending Bastani with marketing theory.

De Freitas connection: Use the Interviewer role to develop the framing conditions. Use the Actor role to pilot-test how different student personas respond to each frame. Use the Designer role to select diverse stimuli across framing conditions.

Bastani connection: Their GPT Base vs. GPT Tutor is a crude framing manipulation. We propose a theoretically grounded framing taxonomy (tutor = authority, collaborator = partner, tool = instrument) with marketing-theory predictions about each.

POCKET 02

The AI Adoption Funnel: Consumer Decision Journey in Educational AI

"What predicts students' adoption, continued use, and abandonment of AI learning tools — and how do marketing-relevant factors (perceived value, brand trust, social proof) interact with educational factors (academic self-concept, prior achievement) to shape the adoption trajectory?"

Why this works: Treats AI tool selection as a consumer decision journey. Maps the AIDA/adoption funnel onto educational technology. Dr. Du's note about students "self-selecting" tools is literally consumer choice behavior. Demographic proxies (race, SES) become moderators of a theoretically specified adoption model.

De Freitas connection: Use productivity techniques (Guideline 1) to generate comprehensive lists of adoption factors. Use semantic breadth (Guideline 2) to discover non-obvious predictors across marketing and education literatures.

adoption funnel consumer journey edtech adoption self-concept survey + behavioral
POCKET 03

Productive Struggle vs. Cognitive Offloading: The Persuasion Mechanism

"Can a brief persuasive intervention — reframing AI difficulty as a signal of learning rather than tool failure — shift students from cognitive offloading (outsourcing thinking) to productive struggle (effortful engagement) with AI tools?"

Why this works: This is a wise intervention study with a persuasion core. The IV is message framing (your ADV 382J expertise). The mechanism is how a reframe changes the meaning students assign to struggle with AI. Bastani's "crutch" finding is the baseline; our intervention is the treatment.

De Freitas connection: Guideline 9 explicitly warns about "de-skilling" — using AI as an answering machine rather than a learning tool. Our intervention directly addresses this. Use Guideline 7 (Interviewer) to develop the intervention language through Socratic probing.

persuasion message framing productive struggle wise intervention RCT
POCKET 04

The Hybrid Method as Mirror: AI Studying AI-in-Education

"What novel insights about student-AI interaction emerge when AI-human hybrid research methods (Arora et al., 2025) are applied to study AI's role in education — and how do these insights differ from traditional survey-based approaches?"

Why this works: The reflexive move — using the method to study the method's domain. This is a methodological contribution paper. It doesn't just apply Arora to education; it asks whether the hybrid method reveals different patterns than traditional methods when the research subject is AI itself. Publishable in JM as a methods extension.

De Freitas connection: All 4 roles apply directly. Designer: select diverse student-AI interaction scenarios. Writer: articulate findings. Interviewer: probe human researchers' interpretations. Actor: simulate student perspectives for pilot testing.

hybrid method reflexive design consumer insight AI education
